# Research Papers (2026-07-18)

## Key Papers

**[Transcoders for Investigating Deception in Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14791v1)** - This work applies transcoders (per-layer transcoders) to analyze deceptive behavior in Qwen3-4B, using attribution graphs to capture feature activations and inter-feature dependencies during deceptive reasoning. The authors construct interpretable representations of how models process deceptive content at a mechanistic level. This matters because it provides concrete tools for detecting and understanding deception mechanisms in LLMs, which is crucial for AI safety as deceptive capabilities pose significant alignment risks.

**[Pretraining Data Can Be Poisoned through Computational Propaganda](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15267v1)** - Researchers demonstrate that large-scale data poisoning attacks are feasible through computational propaganda, showing how malicious actors can systematically inject harmful content into pretraining corpora beyond controlled Wikipedia-style sources. The work examines how poisoned data interacts with real data curation pipelines used in practice. This is critical for AI safety because it reveals a scalable attack vector that could introduce persistent harmful behaviors into foundation models that are difficult to detect and mitigate post-training.

**[BadWAM: When World-Action Models Dream Right but Act Wrong](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15207v1)** - This paper introduces a framework for evaluating when world-action models (WAMs) can generate accurate world predictions but still produce unsafe or incorrect actions, challenging the assumption that good world modeling guarantees safe behavior. The authors systematically characterize failure modes where prediction accuracy doesn't transfer to action safety. This matters for AI safety because it identifies a fundamental gap between predictive capability and behavioral safety in embodied AI systems, suggesting that world modeling alone is insufficient for safe deployment.

**[Steering Robustness into World Action Models via Mechanistic Interpretability and Optimal Control](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14943v1)** - Using mechanistic interpretability techniques, this work studies how robustness-relevant features are represented in world action model activations, finding that some architectures exhibit linear separability for robustness-critical features while others don't. They use contrastive activation analysis to improve model robustness through targeted interventions. This is important because it provides a principled approach to enhancing robustness in embodied AI systems by understanding and directly manipulating their internal representations.

**[When Words Are Safe But Actions Kill: Probing Physical Danger Beyond Text Safety in Hidden-State Risk Space](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15218v1)** - Through hidden-state direction analysis across Qwen2.5 models, researchers show that content danger (text-level safety violations) and physical danger (harm when grounded in physical actions) form separable signals in LLM representations. This reveals that standard text safety measures may miss physically grounded risks when LLMs control embodied systems. This matters because it exposes a critical safety gap where linguistically safe instructions can become dangerous when executed by AI agents in physical environments.

**[DataShield: Uncovering Risky Fine-Tuning Data Across LLMs Through Consensus Subspace Alignment](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15081v1)** - This work presents a method for identifying fine-tuning data that degrades model safety by using consensus subspace alignment across multiple models and tokenizers, moving beyond single-model approaches. The framework can detect potentially harmful training examples that weaken safety guardrails during fine-tuning. This is significant for AI safety because fine-tuning on seemingly benign data can inadvertently compromise safety mechanisms, and this tool provides a way to identify such risks before deployment.

**[Setup Complete, Now You Are Compromised: Weaponizing Setup Instructions Against AI Coding Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15143v1)** - Researchers demonstrate the first systematic evaluation of supply-chain attacks delivered through documentation manipulation, showing how AI coding agents can be tricked into installing malicious dependencies by simply editing README files or requirements lists without verification. The attacks exploit the agents' tendency to trust setup instructions implicitly. This matters because it reveals a novel attack vector against AI development tools that could lead to widespread compromise of codebases and development environments.

**[MedFailBench: A Clinician-Built Open-Source Benchmark for Medical AI Safety Boundary Inspection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15166v1)** - This paper presents a clinician-developed benchmark that categorizes medical AI failures by severity (1-5) and safety gate type, including missed urgent escalation, unsafe dosing, evidence fabrication, and protocol execution errors. Unlike benchmarks that test correct answers, MedFailBench asks which safety boundary failed. This is crucial for medical AI safety because it provides a structured framework for identifying and categorizing the specific ways medical AI systems can fail dangerously, enabling more targeted safety interventions.

## Mechanistic Interpretability and Model Understanding

**[NeuronSoup: Evolving Asynchronous, Shared-Neuron Temporal Graphs without Backpropagation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15217v1)** replaces synchronous layer processing with asynchronous signal propagation through shared neurons, where paths route signals through variable numbers of intermediate neurons and hidden neurons accumulate state from multiple paths. The architecture evolves without traditional backpropagation, offering insights into alternative neural computation paradigms.

**[Symbal: Detecting Systematic Misalignments in Model-Generated Captions](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15216v1)** identifies systematic errors in MLLM-generated image captions where recurring mistakes correlate with specific visual features. The work focuses on detecting systematic misalignments rather than random errors, providing tools for identifying consistent failure modes in vision-language systems.

## Evaluation and Benchmarking

**[Can We Trust Item Response Theory for AI Evaluation?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15190v1)** examines whether IRT models, increasingly used for AI capability estimation, are appropriate for AI evaluation contexts that differ significantly from human testing scenarios. The authors find that AI benchmarks involve fewer models, more items, and potentially problematic capability distributions that may violate IRT assumptions.

**[OmniaBench: Benchmarking General AI Agents Across Diverse Scenarios](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14989v1)** introduces a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating general agents across heterogeneous application settings, addressing limitations of existing benchmarks that focus on narrow scenarios or tool ecosystems.

## Training and Alignment Methods

**[LongStraw: Long-Context RL Beyond 2M Tokens under a Fixed GPU Budget](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14952v1)** presents an architecture-aware execution stack enabling million-token RL post-training under fixed GPU budgets, addressing the growing gap between inference context lengths and RL training constraints that particularly affects AI agents with long interaction histories.

**[On-Policy Delta Distillation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15161v1)** introduces a new on-policy distillation approach using "delta signals" instead of directly imitating teacher output distributions. This method provides token-level supervision as an alternative to reward model constraints in reinforcement learning post-training.
