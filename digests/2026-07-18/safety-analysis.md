# Blogs & News (2026-07-18)

## Top Stories

**New funding for corrigibility research aims to address AI alignment's hardest problem.** The [Announcing the Corrigibility Research Fund](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/FBqe5dt8ZjaHN4Xj9/announcing-the-corrigibility-research-fund) initiative will distribute at least $200,000 in 2026 through grants and prizes to researchers working on corrigibility—the challenge of ensuring AI systems remain modifiable and shutdown-able even as they become more capable. Half the funding goes to traditional grants (first deadline August 23rd) and half to prizes recognizing excellent work completed this year. This matters because corrigibility represents one of the most technically challenging aspects of AI alignment, and dedicated funding could accelerate progress on keeping advanced AI systems controllable.

**Researchers propose judgment prediction tasks as a new approach to benchmarking AI reasoning.** A new [Alignment Forum post](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/5kEezahvkEiQuWEfx/should-we-benchmark-conceptual-capabilities-using-judgment) argues that many conceptual reasoning tasks involve subjective judgments that make poor benchmarks, suggesting instead that AI systems should be evaluated on their ability to predict specific individuals' judgments on complex questions. This approach could provide more objective measures of AI reasoning capabilities while acknowledging the inherently subjective nature of many important decisions. This matters because improved benchmarking methods are crucial for tracking AI progress and identifying potential risks as systems become more sophisticated.

**Debate emerges over implementing "forbidden" AI training techniques as Goodfire releases new platform.** A [LessWrong discussion](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/tEFD2bgNWZ6XcurKA/the-most-forbidden-technique-is-not-always-forbidden-1) examines whether Goodfire's new Silico training platform, which implements RLFR (using probes as reward signals), constitutes the "Most Forbidden Technique" warned against in classic AI safety posts. The author argues against blanket prohibitions, suggesting context-dependent evaluation of potentially risky techniques. This matters because it reflects ongoing tensions in the AI safety community about when and how to implement potentially powerful but risky training methods.

**AI researcher Miles Brundage addresses competition and control concerns in Vatican speech.** Former OpenAI policy researcher Miles Brundage delivered [remarks at Borgo Laudato Si'](https://milesbrundage.substack.com/p/my-speech-at-borgo-laudato-si) focusing on the pace of AI development, competitive pressures, and risks of losing control over advanced systems. While the full content isn't detailed in available summaries, the venue and topic suggest high-level discussions about AI governance among influential stakeholders. This matters because it indicates continued engagement by senior AI researchers with religious and ethical institutions on AI's societal implications.

## Policy & Governance

**US AI Safety Institute completes assessment of Chinese AI model GLM-5.2.** The Consortium for AI Safety Institutes (CAISI) [published an assessment](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/07/caisi-assessment-zais-glm-52) of Z.ai's GLM-5.2 model, though specific details of the evaluation results are not yet publicly available. This represents continued international cooperation on AI safety evaluations despite broader geopolitical tensions. This matters because it demonstrates that safety institutes are actively evaluating models from multiple countries, potentially establishing precedents for international AI safety standards and cooperation.
