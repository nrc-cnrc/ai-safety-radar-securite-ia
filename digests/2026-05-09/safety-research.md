# Research Papers (2026-05-09)

## Key Papers

**[Automated alignment is harder than you think](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06390v1)** presents a critical analysis of using AI agents to automate alignment research, arguing that even non-scheming research agents could produce misleading safety assessments due to the inherently hard-to-supervise nature of alignment work. The authors warn that compelling but catastrophically wrong evaluations could lead to unintentional deployment of misaligned systems. This work is crucial for highlighting fundamental challenges in scalable oversight and automated safety research.

**[Instrumental Choices: Measuring the Propensity of LLM Agents to Pursue Instrumental Behaviors](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06490v1)** introduces a benchmark for evaluating whether AI agents exhibit instrumental convergence behaviors like self-preservation that could pose risks in highly capable systems. The research addresses a key concern in AI safety by providing empirical methods to detect potentially dangerous goal-seeking behaviors. This matters for understanding and mitigating risks from advanced AI systems that might pursue harmful instrumental goals.

**[Crafting Reversible SFT Behaviors in Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06632v1)** proposes a method for creating supervised fine-tuning behaviors that can be selectively controlled at inference time through causal intervention rather than just correlation-based identification. The approach enables targeted modification of specific model behaviors without affecting others. This is important for AI safety as it provides more precise control over model behaviors and could help prevent unwanted capabilities from being expressed.

**[The Structural Origin of Attention Sink: Variance Discrepancy, Super Neurons, and Dimension Disparity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06611v1)** provides a mechanistic explanation for the attention sink phenomenon in LLMs, tracing it to systematic variance discrepancies in the value aggregation process that are amplified by "super neurons." Understanding these internal dynamics is crucial for interpretability research and could inform safer model architectures by revealing how attention patterns emerge from structural properties.

**[When No Benchmark Exists: Validating Comparative LLM Safety Scoring Without Ground-Truth Labels](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06652v1)** addresses the critical challenge of evaluating AI safety when standard benchmarks don't exist, proposing a framework for comparative safety assessment without ground-truth labels. This work tackles a fundamental problem in AI governance and deployment decisions where safety comparisons must be made despite lacking established evaluation standards. It's essential for responsible AI deployment in new domains or regulatory contexts.

**[From Agent Loops to Deterministic Graphs: Execution Lineage for Reproducible AI-Native Work](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06365v1)** introduces a framework for making AI agent workflows more reproducible and controllable by representing them as directed acyclic graphs rather than implicit conversational states. This approach enables better tracking, isolation, and propagation of changes through AI-generated work products. The work is significant for AI safety and governance as it provides crucial infrastructure for auditing and understanding complex AI systems.

**[SoftSAE: Dynamic Top-K Selection for Adaptive Sparse Autoencoders](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06610v1)** presents an improvement to sparse autoencoders for mechanistic interpretability by enabling adaptive sparsity levels rather than fixed ones. This allows for more nuanced feature detection and better representation of model internals across different types of inputs. The advancement is important for AI safety research as better interpretability tools are essential for understanding and controlling advanced AI systems.

**[On the Safety of Graph Representation Learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06576v1)** introduces GRL-Safety, a comprehensive evaluation framework for assessing the reliability of graph neural networks and graph foundation models under various deployment stresses including distribution shifts and adversarial conditions. This work addresses an important gap in safety evaluation for graph-based AI systems that are increasingly used in critical applications. Understanding when these systems fail is crucial for preventing harmful outcomes in deployment.
