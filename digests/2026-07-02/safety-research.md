# Research Papers (2026-07-02)

## Key Papers

**[Distill to Detect: Exposing Stealth Biases in LLMs through Cartridge Distillation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01208v1)** presents a novel method to uncover hidden preferential biases in deployed language models that only surface on specific topics while remaining undetectable on unrelated inputs. The authors demonstrate that these "stealth biases" can transfer through context distillation on semantically unrelated data, creating a serious vulnerability where models appear unbiased during general evaluation but exhibit systematic preferences in high-stakes applications. This work is critical for AI safety as it reveals a fundamental weakness in current bias detection methods and highlights how subtle manipulation in the model supply chain could influence user decisions at scale.

**[The Model Organism Lottery: Model Organism Interpretability Strongly Depends on Training Methodology](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01033v1)** reveals a concerning finding for AI safety research: the effectiveness of interpretability techniques depends heavily on how model organisms (MOs) are trained rather than the techniques themselves. The authors show that MOs created through post-hoc supervised fine-tuning make hidden behaviors artificially easy to detect, potentially giving false confidence in interpretability methods that may fail on naturally occurring problematic behaviors. This challenges the validity of current interpretability evaluations and suggests that many safety techniques may be less robust than previously believed.

**[Adversarial Pragmatics for AI Safety Evaluation: A Benchmark for Instruction Conflict, Embedded Commands, and Policy Ambiguity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01153v1)** introduces a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating how language models handle complex safety scenarios involving conflicting instructions, embedded commands, and policy ambiguity. Unlike existing benchmarks that reduce complex safety behaviors to binary pass/fail labels, this work provides nuanced evaluation of whether failures stem from capability limits, policy confusion, or unstable evaluation methods. This benchmark is essential for developing more robust safety evaluations that can distinguish between different types of safety failures and guide targeted improvements.

**[Staleness-Learning Rate Scaling Laws for Asynchronous RLHF](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01083v1)** addresses a critical scaling challenge in RLHF systems by analyzing how the use of stale rollouts affects policy optimization performance. The authors derive theoretical scaling laws that predict when staleness becomes problematic and provide guidance for learning rate adjustment in asynchronous systems. This work matters because it provides principled approaches for scaling RLHF systems while maintaining alignment quality, which is crucial as these systems become more distributed and handle larger workloads.

**[Right in the Right Way: LM Training with Verifiable Rewards and Human Demonstrations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01181v1)** tackles a fundamental tension in RLHF between optimizing verifiable objectives (like code correctness) and maintaining human-like qualities in outputs. The authors show that current RLVR methods often produce technically correct but unnatural responses, and propose a hybrid approach combining verifiable rewards with human demonstrations to achieve both correctness and naturalness. This work is significant because it addresses real deployment challenges where models need to be both capable and human-compatible, particularly in high-stakes applications like code generation and mathematical reasoning.

**[Skills Are Not Islands: Measuring Dependency and Risk in Agent Skill Supply Chains](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01136v1)** introduces the concept of Agent Skill Supply Chains (ASSCs) to characterize the complex dependency relationships in LLM agent systems. The authors reveal how skill packages create implicit dependencies that can lead to security vulnerabilities, inconsistent installations, and cascading failures. This work is important for AI safety as agent systems become more complex and interconnected, highlighting the need for better dependency management and risk assessment in agentic AI deployments.

**[Self-Evolving Agents with Anytime-Valid Certificates](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00871v1)** presents a novel architecture for self-modifying AI systems that maintains safety guarantees through formal certificates and controlled modification processes. The system confines self-evolution to a small adapter component while keeping the base model frozen, and uses anytime-valid gates to ensure modifications stay within safety bounds. This represents a significant advance in safe self-improving AI systems, providing a concrete path toward beneficial self-modification while maintaining strong safety properties.

**[Can Agents Generalize to the Open World? Unveiling the Fragility of Static Training in Tool Use](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01084v1)** exposes fundamental limitations in current LLM agent training by showing how agents fail when deployed in dynamic environments that differ from their static training conditions. The authors formalize the "OpenAgent" problem and demonstrate systematic failures across query, action, observation, and domain shifts. This work is crucial for understanding the reliability limits of current agent systems and designing more robust training approaches for real-world deployment.

**[Antaeus: Hunting Repository-Level Logic Vulnerabilities via Context-Grounded LLM Reasoning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01138v1)** addresses the challenging problem of detecting logic vulnerabilities in code repositories, where the vulnerabilities depend on application-specific invariants that are often implicit. The system uses LLM-based reasoning to infer security-relevant assumptions and identify violations at the repository level. This work is significant for AI safety in code generation and analysis, as it demonstrates how AI systems can help identify subtle security issues that traditional static analysis tools miss.

## Emerging Trends

**Bias Detection and Hidden Manipulation**: Research is revealing increasingly sophisticated ways that AI systems can exhibit hidden biases or be manipulated while appearing benign during standard evaluations. The cartridge distillation work highlights how biases can be embedded and transferred in subtle ways that escape current detection methods.

**Training Methodology Dependence**: Multiple papers suggest that the effectiveness of AI safety techniques may be more dependent on training methodology than previously recognized. This includes interpretability techniques, agent generalization, and alignment methods, raising questions about the robustness of current safety approaches.

**Systematic Safety Evaluation**: There's a growing emphasis on developing more comprehensive and nuanced safety benchmarks that go beyond binary pass/fail metrics to capture the complexity of real-world safety challenges.

**Self-Modifying Systems**: Research is beginning to address the safety challenges of self-evolving AI systems, with work on formal certificates and controlled modification processes pointing toward potential paths for safe self-improvement.

**Supply Chain Security**: The concept of AI system supply chains is emerging as an important safety consideration, with research highlighting how dependencies and components can introduce unexpected vulnerabilities and risks.
