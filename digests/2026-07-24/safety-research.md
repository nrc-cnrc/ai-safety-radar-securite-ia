# Research Papers (2026-07-24)

## Key Papers

[**The Boundaries of Automation: A Theory of Persistent Human Participation**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21547v1) challenges the assumption that humans remain in the loop only because AI isn't capable enough yet, arguing instead for conceptual limits to automation even with highly capable systems. The authors propose a theoretical framework for understanding where human participation may persist despite technological advances. This work provides crucial theoretical grounding for AI safety discussions about human-AI collaboration and the limits of autonomous systems.

[**Regulating autonomous and agentic AI**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21345v1) addresses the governance challenges posed by autonomous AI systems, noting that traditional regulatory assumptions about knowledge and control no longer hold when much of the decision-making lies elsewhere in the AI supply chain. The paper argues for bringing the entire AI supply chain within regulatory scope and developing fresh governance approaches. This represents essential policy research for managing the risks of increasingly autonomous AI systems.

[**Token Budget Saturation and Mechanistic Early Detection of Reasoning Non-Convergence in Chain-of-Thought Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21433v1) identifies a bimodal pattern in chain-of-thought reasoning where models either converge to solutions within token budgets (90.3% accuracy) or fail to converge (6.6% accuracy). The researchers demonstrate early detection of non-convergence using internal activations, potentially enabling more efficient reasoning systems. This mechanistic understanding could improve the reliability and efficiency of reasoning-based AI systems.

[**V-DEAL: Diagnosing Video Safety De-Calibration as an Understanding-Refusal Coupling Failure**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21151v1) reveals a counterintuitive vulnerability in video-language models where harmful videos paired with benign queries achieve higher attack success rates than when paired with explicitly harmful queries. The three-level diagnostic framework exposes how safety alignment can fail through understanding-refusal coupling problems. This finding highlights critical safety vulnerabilities in multimodal AI systems that could be exploited maliciously.

[**GuardianAgentBench: Where Agents Fail and How to Guard Them**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.20982v1) presents a comprehensive benchmark of 580 scenarios across six domains, revealing that even state-of-the-art models achieve only 40-60% success rates on safety-critical agent tasks. The benchmark incorporates multi-stage validation and adversarial attacks to expose failure modes in production-ready frameworks. This work provides essential infrastructure for evaluating and improving the safety of autonomous AI agents.

[**Unlearning Under Imbalance: Benchmarking Fairness in Multimodal LLM Unlearning**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21300v1) demonstrates that machine unlearning methods exhibit significant fairness issues when unlearning requests come disproportionately from certain demographic groups, potentially amplifying societal biases. The research shows that demographic imbalance in unlearning requests can worsen model performance on underrepresented groups. This highlights critical fairness considerations for privacy-preserving AI systems.

[**AREX: Towards a Recursively Self-Improving Agent for Deep Research**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21461v1) introduces a research agent that recursively improves its answers by verifying intermediate results and using partial verification to guide refinement, rather than simply searching longer. The discovery-verification asymmetry approach shows promise for complex reasoning tasks requiring multiple constraint satisfaction. This represents progress toward more sophisticated AI research assistants that could accelerate scientific discovery.

[**QuantiBias: Benchmarking Quantization-Induced Bias in LLMs**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.21063v1) reveals that quantization—a standard efficiency technique applied to nearly every deployed LLM—significantly increases bias while maintaining performance on standard safety evaluations. The paper shows that quantized models exhibit increased bias that goes undetected by current evaluation methods. This finding exposes a critical blind spot in AI safety evaluation that affects virtually all production language models.
