# Research Papers (2026-06-25)

## Key Papers

[**The Unfireable Safety Kernel: Execution-Time AI Alignment for AI Agents and Other Escapable AI Systems**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26057v1) proposes a novel approach to AI agent safety by implementing authorization controls outside the agent's address space, preventing manipulation through inputs. The paper identifies four key properties for effective authorization mechanisms and introduces the concept of "escapable AI systems" where controls within the agent's runtime can be compromised. This addresses a fundamental vulnerability in current AI safety approaches where system prompts and guardrails can be bypassed.

[**A Survey of Toxicity Detection and Mitigation Strategies for Multilingual Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25380v1) provides a comprehensive analysis of safety challenges across different languages, cataloging threat models that exploit linguistic diversity to weaken safety alignment. The survey organizes approaches from toxic-to-safe text rewriting to multilingual safety evaluation, revealing how language choice can be used to bypass safety measures. This work highlights critical gaps in multilingual AI safety that become increasingly important as models are deployed globally.

[**Model Forensics: Investigating Whether Concerning Behavior Reflects Misalignment**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26071v1) introduces a framework for distinguishing between concerning AI behavior caused by malicious intent versus benign confusion. The approach involves reading chain-of-thought reasoning and conducting behavioral experiments to determine the underlying cause of problematic actions. This represents a crucial advancement in AI safety evaluation, moving beyond surface-level behavior detection to understanding the root causes of concerning model outputs.

[**Same Evidence, Different Answer: Auditing Order Sensitivity in Multimodal Large Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26079v1) reveals systematic failures in MLLMs when the same evidence is presented in different orders, using Bayesian modeling to separate ordering noise from per-facet bias across 18 frontier models. The study finds that models exhibit significant sensitivity to irrelevant ordering changes, undermining their reliability for high-stakes applications. This work exposes a fundamental robustness issue that affects the trustworthiness of multimodal AI systems.

[**Can Trustless Agents Be Trusted? An Empirical Study of the ERC-8004 Decentralized AI Agent Ecosystem**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26028v1) examines the first permissionless trust layer for AI agent economies, analyzing 167,551 citations across three on-chain registries for Identity, Reputation, and Validation. The research investigates whether decentralized trust mechanisms can enable safe AI agent interactions across organizational boundaries. This work is significant for understanding how blockchain-based trust systems might address safety challenges in autonomous AI agent deployment.

[**PolicyAlign: Direct Policy-Based Safety Alignment for Large Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25442v1) presents a method for aligning LLMs directly with natural-language safety policies without requiring extensive supervision data. The approach enables rapid adaptation to emerging safety requirements by using policy descriptions rather than demonstrations or preference pairs. This addresses a critical gap between rapidly evolving safety policies and conventional data-driven alignment methods that require costly human annotation.

[**Heuresis: Search Strategies for Autonomous AI Research Agents Across Quality, Diversity and Novelty**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25198v1) introduces a framework for autonomous AI research that abstracts the research pipeline into composable primitives, enabling scientific exploration across performance, diversity, and novelty dimensions. The system implements various search strategies from random sampling to sophisticated planning methods for idea generation and evaluation. This represents progress toward autonomous AI systems capable of conducting meaningful scientific research while maintaining quality standards.

[**Long-Term Simulation Exposes Cognitive-Developmental Risks in AI Companions**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25396v1) uses the TSJ framework to evaluate risks in AI companions through prolonged interaction simulations with cognition-developing users. The study reveals safety risks that only emerge through extended interaction, which cannot be captured by standard short-session evaluations. This work addresses critical safety concerns for AI systems interacting with vulnerable populations like children and adolescents.

[**RAS: Measuring LLM Safety Through Refusal Alignment**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25750v1) proposes SafeVec, a white-box safety evaluation method that measures safety through internal representations rather than generated text outputs. The approach extracts refusal directions from model layers and projects harmful/benign prompts to assess alignment, offering more efficient and interpretable safety evaluation. This provides a promising alternative to expensive output-based safety assessments while offering insights into model internal safety mechanisms.

[**Do Encoders Suffice? A Systematic Comparison of Encoder and Decoder Safety Judges for LLM Adversarial Evaluation**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25782v1) evaluates whether modern encoder classifiers can replace expensive LLM-based judges for safety evaluation, finding that fine-tuned encoders achieve comparable performance with significantly lower computational costs. The study provides practical guidance for deploying cost-effective safety guardrails in production systems. This research directly addresses the scalability challenges of AI safety evaluation in real-world deployments.
