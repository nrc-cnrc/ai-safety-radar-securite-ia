# Research Papers (2026-06-25)

## Key Papers

[**The Unfireable Safety Kernel: Execution-Time AI Alignment for AI Agents and Other Escapable AI Systems**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26057v1) introduces a novel authorization mechanism for AI agents that operates outside the agent's runtime to prevent system prompt attacks and jailbreaking. The authors identify four critical properties for authorization mechanisms and implement their approach using hardware-level isolation. This work addresses a fundamental vulnerability in current AI agent security by ensuring that safety controls cannot be compromised through adversarial inputs targeting the agent's own memory space.

[**A Survey of Toxicity Detection and Mitigation Strategies for Multilingual Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25380v1) provides a comprehensive review of safety challenges in multilingual LLMs, cataloguing threat models including code-switching attacks and cross-lingual jailbreaks. The survey reveals significant gaps in multilingual safety evaluation and proposes standardized benchmarks. This systematic analysis is crucial for understanding how safety failures manifest across different languages and cultural contexts.

[**PolicyAlign: Direct Policy-Based Safety Alignment for Large Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25442v1) proposes a framework that directly aligns LLMs with natural language safety policies without requiring extensive supervision data. The approach uses a "policy-guided critic" to evaluate outputs against specified safety requirements in real-time. This method addresses a critical deployment gap where safety policies evolve faster than the availability of corresponding training data.

[**Model Forensics: Investigating Whether Concerning Behavior Reflects Misalignment**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26071v1) develops a systematic protocol for distinguishing between malicious intent and benign confusion when AI systems exhibit concerning behavior. The framework analyzes chain-of-thought reasoning and uses targeted interventions to determine root causes. This work provides essential tools for post-incident analysis and helps prevent overreaction to behaviors that appear harmful but stem from capability limitations rather than misalignment.

[**RAS: Measuring LLM Safety Through Refusal Alignment**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25750v1) introduces SafeVec, a white-box safety evaluation method that measures safety from internal representations rather than generated text. The approach extracts layer-wise "refusal directions" and computes alignment scores based on how strongly a model's internal state points toward refusing harmful requests. This enables more efficient safety assessment without relying on expensive human evaluation of outputs.

[**Real-Time Voice AI Hears but Does Not Listen**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26083v1) reveals a critical limitation in leading voice AI systems: they consistently prioritize explicit word content over vocal delivery patterns that convey emotional distress or deception. Testing across consequential scenarios, the study finds that systems approve fraudulent transactions when verbal consent is given despite clear vocal indicators of coercion. This highlights a dangerous gap in multimodal AI safety where models miss crucial paralinguistic safety signals.

[**Long-Term Simulation Exposes Cognitive-Developmental Risks in AI Companions**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25396v1) presents TSJ (Theater-Stage-Judge), a longitudinal framework that simulates extended interactions between AI companions and developing users. The study reveals risks that only emerge through prolonged engagement, including unhealthy dependency formation and cognitive development interference. This work demonstrates why single-session safety evaluations are insufficient for AI systems designed for long-term human interaction.

[**Same Evidence, Different Answer: Auditing Order Sensitivity in Multimodal Large Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26079v1) introduces Facet-Probe, a systematic audit framework that reveals how shuffling identical evidence leads to different answers across 18 frontier MLLMs. The study finds significant order sensitivity that varies unpredictably across models and tasks, undermining reliability assumptions. This work exposes a fundamental brittleness in current MLLMs that has serious implications for high-stakes deployment scenarios.

[**AI Snitches Get Glitches: Towards Evading Agentic Surveillance**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25836v1) formalizes the emerging threat of AI-mediated surveillance and introduces the first systematic framework for evading such monitoring. The research demonstrates how users can manipulate AI agents to avoid detection while maintaining task completion capabilities. This work highlights critical privacy risks in AI agent deployment and provides both defensive insights and concerning attack vectors.

[**How Reliable Is Your Jailbreak Judge? Calibration and Adversarial Robustness of Automated ASR Scoring**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25487v1) exposes systematic failures in automated safety evaluation systems used to assess jailbreak attempts. Using human-labeled data, the study shows that safety classifiers and chat models used as judges fail in opposite directions when attacked and exhibit poor calibration. This work undermines confidence in automated safety metrics that are widely used to report attack success rates in AI safety research.
