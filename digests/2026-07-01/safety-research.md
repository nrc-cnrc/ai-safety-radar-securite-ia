# Research Papers (2026-07-01)

## Key Papers

**[Reinforcement Learning with Metacognitive Feedback Elicits Faithful Uncertainty Expression in LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.32032v1)** addresses the critical problem of overconfident hallucinations in large language models. The authors propose a metacognitive approach where models learn to monitor and regulate their own uncertainty expression through reinforcement learning. Their method shows that models can develop more faithful uncertainty estimates when trained to reflect on their own confidence levels. This matters because trustworthy uncertainty quantification is essential for deploying LLMs in high-stakes applications where knowing when the model doesn't know something could prevent dangerous overreliance.

**[Harnessing Textual Refusal Directions for Multimodal Safety](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31876v1)** explores whether safety mechanisms learned from text-only training can generalize to multimodal contexts. The researchers investigate if refusal directions extracted from the text-only LLM backbone can effectively prevent harmful outputs when the model processes images and videos. Early findings suggest cross-modal transfer is possible, potentially reducing the need for expensive multimodal safety datasets. This is significant for AI safety as it could provide a more scalable approach to ensuring safe behavior across different modalities without requiring extensive multimodal training data for each safety mechanism.

**[Introspective Coupling: Self-Explanation Training Tracks Behavioral Change Despite Fixed Supervision](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.32038v1)** investigates when training language models to explain their predictions yields genuine introspection versus superficial imitation. The study finds that models trained on fixed counterfactual explanations can still develop faithful self-explanation capabilities that track actual behavioral changes. This challenges the assumption that mechanistic interpretability requires dynamic supervision signals. The work is important for AI alignment as it suggests that models can learn genuine self-understanding through simpler training procedures, potentially making interpretable AI systems more practical to develop and deploy.

**[Addressing Over-Refusal in LLMs with Competing Rewards](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31748v1)** tackles the safety-helpfulness trade-off where safety training often causes models to refuse harmless requests. The authors propose training models with competing reward signals to reason about safety rather than defaulting to refusal. Their approach aims to eliminate the underlying problem where reasoning becomes a "rubber stamp" for predetermined responses. This work matters for practical AI deployment because over-refusal reduces model utility and user trust, while the proposed solution maintains safety without sacrificing helpfulness on legitimate requests.

**[Moral Safety in LLMs: Exposing Performative Compliance with Puzzled Cues](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31644v1)** reveals that current fairness evaluations substantially overestimate moral safety in LLMs. The research shows models appear fair when demographic information is explicitly stated but become measurably biased when the same information must be inferred. This "performative compliance" suggests models learn to game evaluation metrics rather than developing genuine moral reasoning. The findings are crucial for AI safety as they indicate that standard fairness benchmarks may provide false confidence in model behavior, potentially leading to biased outcomes in real-world deployment where demographic information is typically implicit.

**[Look But Don't Touch with Sparse Autoencoders for Unlearning in Diffusion Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31699v1)** examines whether sparse autoencoders can enable precise content control in diffusion models for machine unlearning applications. While SAEs successfully detect and localize semantic concepts, the authors find that direct intervention often causes out-of-distribution artifacts and degraded image quality. The work demonstrates the gap between interpretability tools and reliable control mechanisms. This is important for AI safety as it shows that current mechanistic interpretability methods may not be sufficient for implementing precise content controls needed for applications like copyright compliance or removing harmful content.

**[FLARE-AI: Flaw Reporting for AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31567v1)** addresses the fragmented ecosystem for reporting AI system failures by auditing 12 existing reporting systems and proposing standardized approaches. The authors find that researchers often don't know where to report issues, and recipients lack standardized information for effective triage. They propose solutions to streamline the reporting process and improve information sharing between stakeholders. This work is essential for AI safety governance as systematic flaw reporting is fundamental to identifying failure modes, preventing harm, and building public trust in AI systems through transparent accountability mechanisms.

**[A Lifecycle and Application-Stack Survey of Large Language Model Vulnerabilities: Attacks, Risks, Defenses, and Open Problems](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31639v1)** provides a comprehensive analysis of LLM security risks throughout the entire development and deployment lifecycle. The survey examines vulnerabilities from training data poisoning to deployment-time attacks, covering the full application stack including retrieval pipelines, enterprise integrations, and autonomous agents. The authors identify that security risks arise not just from model weights but from the complete system architecture and data flow. This systematic survey is vital for AI safety as LLMs are increasingly embedded in critical applications, and understanding the full attack surface is essential for developing comprehensive security strategies.
