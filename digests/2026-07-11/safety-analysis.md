# Blogs & News (2026-07-11)

## Top Stories

The AI Futures Project released [AI 2040: Plan A](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/pFzctpJBat95SrCyC/ai-2040-plan-a), a comprehensive scenario outlining how humanity could delay superintelligence development until 2040 to ensure safer outcomes. The plan represents their recommendation rather than prediction, proposing coordinated global governance to manage the intelligence explosion more carefully. This matters because it provides one of the most detailed roadmaps yet for how the AI safety community believes we could navigate the critical period before AGI.

A new post on value generalization explores [value correction](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/iPyJfD9Jyxj6Jfdws/value-generalisation-value-correction) in reinforcement learning, where an AI agent recognizes its current reward function is likely incorrect and acts to fix it. The research demonstrates how agents might learn to identify and correct misaligned objectives when moving from training to deployment scenarios. This matters because value correction could be a key mechanism for building AI systems that remain aligned even when their initial training was imperfect.

Researchers examined the robustness of [natural language autoencoders](https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/LQXWiF8PyJ5ojNsEv/how-robust-are-natural-language-autoencoders-to) (NLAs) to different initialization methods, finding that Qwen2.5-7B NLAs show some resilience to irrelevant statements and sentiment changes in training data. The work tests how sensitive these interpretability tools are to variations in the human-labeled training data used to teach them to describe model internals. This matters because NLAs are becoming important tools for understanding what AI systems are thinking, and their reliability depends on robust performance across different training conditions.

Critics of Plan A highlight a ["dry tinder" problem](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/8iDZnQwmvwuxZo3Wx/plan-a-s-problem-with-dry-tinder), arguing that the plan's proposed infrastructure for safely developing superintelligence could itself become a vulnerability if control is lost. The critique suggests that building powerful AI governance institutions creates new attack surfaces that could accelerate rather than slow dangerous AI development. This matters because it identifies potential failure modes in coordinated AI governance approaches that could undermine safety efforts.

Ajeya Cotra and Kelsey Piper argue for [total research transparency](https://www.planned-obsolescence.org/p/total-research-transparency-would) in AI development, suggesting complete openness about research directions and findings could help identify dangerous paths before they're pursued. They propose that transparency could enable the community to collectively recognize and avoid risky research directions. This matters because it represents a potential alternative to current approaches that balance openness with security concerns, though it raises significant questions about competitive dynamics and dual-use risks.
