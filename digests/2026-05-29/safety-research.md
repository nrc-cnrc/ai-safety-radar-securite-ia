# Research Papers (2026-05-29)

## Key Papers

The most significant development this week is Anthropic's breakthrough work on [Scaling Monosemanticity: Extracting Interpretable Features from Claude 3 Sonnet](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29358v1). Researchers successfully trained sparse autoencoders with up to 34 million features on Claude 3 Sonnet's middle layer, demonstrating that dictionary learning methods can extract interpretable features from production-scale language models. The resulting features are multilingual and multimodal, generalizing to images despite text-only training. This represents a major advance in mechanistic interpretability, providing the first scalable method for understanding the internal representations of large language models used in practice.

[LLMSurgeon: Diagnosing Data Mixture of Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30348v1) introduces a novel approach to auditing LLM training data by estimating the domain-level distribution of pretraining corpora from generated text alone. The method formalizes "Data Mixture Surgery" to reverse-engineer training data composition, which is critical for understanding model behaviors and failure modes. This addresses a key transparency challenge in AI safety, as training data composition shapes model capabilities but is rarely disclosed by developers.

[How Reliable Are AI Attackers Against a Fixed Vulnerable Target? A 400-Run Empirical Study of LLM Penetration Testing Consistency](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30096v1) presents the first large-scale measurement of LLM attack consistency across 400 autonomous penetration testing runs. The study found significant variability in attack success rates and strategies even when holding the target and prompts constant, revealing fundamental inconsistencies in LLM offensive capabilities. This work provides crucial empirical data for understanding the reliability and predictability of AI-enabled cyber attacks.

[Hijacking Agent Memory: Stealthy Trojan Attacks Through Conversational Interaction](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29960v1) identifies a novel attack vector against LLM agents that leverage long-term memory systems. The research demonstrates how adversaries can inject malicious information through natural conversation to influence future agent behavior, exploiting the selective extraction and rewriting stages in memory pipelines. This highlights a critical security vulnerability as agents become more autonomous and memory-dependent.

[Opir: Efficient Multi-Task Safety Classification for Toxicity, Jailbreaks, Hate Speech, and Harmful Content](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29659v1) introduces a family of encoder-based guardrail models for real-time safety filtering of LLM applications. The models can detect unsafe prompts, toxic language, jailbreak attempts, and harmful responses while distinguishing benign sensitive content from genuinely harmful material. This work addresses the practical need for efficient safety mechanisms that can operate at scale without the computational overhead of large guardrail models.

[MINDGAMES: A Live Arena for Evaluating Social and Strategic Reasoning in Multi-Agent LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29512v1) creates a comprehensive evaluation platform for assessing LLM agents' capacity for sustained social and strategic reasoning across multiple games. The platform operationalizes complementary reasoning demands that static benchmarks cannot capture, providing insights into how well LLMs can handle complex multi-agent interactions. This fills a crucial gap in understanding LLM capabilities for real-world deployment scenarios involving multiple agents.

[Formalizing Mathematics at Scale](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29955v1) presents AutoformBot, a multi-agent system that orchestrates thousands of LLM agents to translate informal textbook mathematics into machine-checked Lean 4 proofs. The system successfully processed 26 open-access textbooks across multiple mathematical domains, demonstrating the potential for automated formalization of mathematical knowledge. This represents significant progress toward AI systems that can rigorously verify and extend mathematical reasoning.
