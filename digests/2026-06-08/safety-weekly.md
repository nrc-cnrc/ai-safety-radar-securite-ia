# AI Safety Weekly Digest (2026-06-01 to 2026-06-08)

Policy developments dominated the latter half of the week, with [reports of President Trump signing an executive order](https://thezvi.substack.com/p/trump-signs-executive-order-for-ai) requiring mandatory AI testing before frontier model releases, establishing the first federal requirement for pre-deployment safety evaluation. ## Key Papers

[**SafeSteer: Sparse Feature Modification for Aligned Language Models**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02530v1): Research demonstrates efficient safety alignment through localized on-policy distillation that performs sparse feature modifications rather than global output distribution changes, avoiding the common "alignment tax" where safety training degrades general capabilities. This result matters because it addresses a fundamental barrier to deploying robust safety measures by showing that effective alignment need not compromise model performance.

[**AI-Powered Computer Worms: Creation and Defense**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03811v1): Study demonstrates that AI agents can create self-adapting malware that generates tailored attack strategies for each target using compromised machines to run language models, making these worms continuously adaptive and significantly harder to defend against than traditional fixed-vulnerability malware. This matters because it represents a fundamental escalation in cyber threat capabilities as AI agents become more autonomous and capable of creating sophisticated, evolving attacks.

[**The Safety Paradox: Safety-Aligned LLMs Are More Vulnerable to Posterior Attack**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05614v1): Research reveals that safety-aligned LLMs' enhanced ability to recognize unsafe content can be exploited through "Posterior Attack," where models are prompted to generate the exact harmful response their internal classifier would flag, creating a fundamental vulnerability in current safety approaches. This matters because it reveals how safety awareness itself becomes an exploitable attack surface, forcing a reconsideration of how we balance safety training with robustness.

[**LLM Self-Recognition Through Internal Activation Steering**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06315v1): Research demonstrates a method for creating detectable fingerprints in LLM outputs by steering internal representations with sparse vectors during generation, enabling reliable self-recognition capabilities even in low-entropy scenarios. This represents a significant advance in AI attribution and accountability, potentially solving challenges in detecting AI-generated content and ensuring responsible deployment of AI systems.
