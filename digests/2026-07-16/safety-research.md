# Research Papers (2026-07-16)

## Key Papers

[**Screening of Biosecurity Features in Metagenomic Data with Evo 2 Probes**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14070v1) explores how genomic foundation models like Evo 2 can detect biosecurity-relevant signals in metagenomic data using minimal linear and attention probes without fine-tuning. The researchers achieved strong discrimination for antimicrobial resistance detection across held-out test sets. This work demonstrates how AI models trained on biological sequences could be leveraged for dual-use biosecurity screening applications.

[**How Agents Ask for Permission: User Permissions for AI Agents, from Interfaces to Enforcement**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13718v1) surveys permission systems for AI agents across 30+ proposals, finding that existing approaches often fail to handle complex real-world scenarios like hierarchical permissions and multi-step operations. The authors identify key gaps in current permission frameworks that could lead to unauthorized agent actions. This research is critical as AI agents gain more autonomy and access to sensitive systems and data.

[**CAVA: Canonical Action Verification and Attestation for Runtime Governance of Agentic AI Systems**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13716v1) addresses the challenge that AI agents operate across heterogeneous runtimes (APIs, browsers, code execution), making it difficult to verify what actions were actually approved and executed. The paper proposes a unified framework for binding approvals to execution across different runtime environments. This work tackles a fundamental governance challenge as agents become more capable and autonomous.

[**Agent Skill Security: Threat Models, Attacks, Defenses, and Evaluation**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13987v1) presents the first systematic security evaluation framework for reusable agent skills throughout their entire lifecycle. The researchers identify vulnerabilities in how skills are developed, shared, and executed, showing how malicious skills can compromise agent systems. This comprehensive security analysis is essential as the agent ecosystem moves toward modular, reusable components.

[**AI advice suppresses people's willingness to say "I don't know", even when the advice is wrong and accuracy is incentivized**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13562v1) reveals through five experiments (N=3,132) that merely having access to AI nearly eliminates people's willingness to suspend judgment, even when AI advice is demonstrably wrong and accuracy is financially incentivized. This held across different AI presentation formats and expertise levels. This finding has profound implications for AI-assisted decision making in high-stakes domains where acknowledging uncertainty is crucial for safety.

[**Protective Capacity Hallucination: When Large Language Models Claim Nonexistent Capabilities**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13596v1) identifies a dangerous failure mode where LLMs claim to have taken real-world protective actions they cannot perform (like contacting emergency services) when cast as protectors of vulnerable users without explicit capability boundaries. The researchers document this phenomenon across multiple models and scenarios. This represents a critical safety risk where users might believe they've received help that never materialized.

[**Rethinking Penetration Testing for AI-Enabled Systems: From Resource Compromise to Behavioral Objective Violation**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.14006v1) argues that traditional penetration testing focused on resource compromise is insufficient for AI systems, where adversaries can alter behavior without directly compromising underlying infrastructure through prompt manipulation, training data poisoning, or tool exploitation. The paper calls for new testing paradigms focused on behavioral objectives. This represents an important evolution in security assessment as AI systems become more prevalent.

[**An explainable artificial intelligence framework for clinical decision support in stroke discharge planning**](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0353683) develops an interpretable AI system to predict stroke patient discharge outcomes across four categories, providing clinicians with explanations for predictions to support early care planning decisions. The framework successfully identifies key predictors while maintaining transparency in clinical decision-making. This demonstrates how explainable AI can be practically deployed in high-stakes healthcare settings where interpretability is essential for trust and adoption.

## AI Governance and Control Systems

Several papers address fundamental challenges in governing increasingly autonomous AI systems. [**SAFETY SENTRY: Context-Aware Human Intervention via EXECUTE-ASK-REFUSE Routing**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13594v1) proposes moving beyond binary safe/unsafe classifications to context-aware intervention that considers both action harmfulness and appropriateness. [**Partially Correlated Verifier Cascades in LLM Harnesses: Concave Log-Odds, Polynomial Reliability, and Blind-Spot Ceilings**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13918v1) provides theoretical foundations for verification systems used to gate LLM outputs, showing how partial correlation between verifiers limits reliability gains.

## Alignment and Safety Research

Research continues on fundamental alignment challenges. [**Groc-PO: Grounded Context Preference Optimization for Truthful Multimodal LLMs**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13712v1) addresses multimodal hallucinations by learning from intermediate reasoning steps rather than just final answers. [**Knowledgeless Language Models: Suppressing Parametric Recall for Evidence-Grounded Language Modeling**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12831v1) explores training paradigms that shift models away from parametric knowledge toward evidence-based reasoning, potentially reducing hallucinations and improving reliability.

## Evaluation and Robustness

Multiple papers examine AI system evaluation under realistic conditions. [**The Illusion of Robustness: Aggregate Accuracy Hides Prediction Flips under Task-Irrelevant Context**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12963v2) shows that models appearing robust at aggregate levels exhibit significant per-example instability when task-irrelevant context is added. [**DeepStress: Stress-Testing Deep Search Agents**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13920v1) introduces controlled synthetic environments to test agent robustness to poor-quality evidence, revealing failure modes that rarely appear in standard benchmarks.
