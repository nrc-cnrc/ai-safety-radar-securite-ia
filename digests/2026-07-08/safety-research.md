# Research Papers (2026-07-08)

## Key Papers

**[Doomed from the Start: Early Abort of LLM Agent Episodes via a Recall-Controlled Probe Cascade](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06503v1)** presents a novel approach to detect agent trajectory failure using lightweight probes on hidden activations. The method can predict episode failure as early as the first interaction round, potentially saving substantial computational resources during agent deployment. This represents an important step toward more efficient LLM agent systems that avoid pursuing doomed trajectories.

**[DT-Guard: Intent-Driven Reasoning-Active Training for Reasoning-Free LLM Safety Guardrail](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06326v1)** addresses the critical trade-off between safety guardrail efficiency and robustness by introducing a novel training methodology. The approach combines classification-based efficiency with reasoning-based accuracy, producing guardrails that can handle concealed intent and ambiguous semantics while maintaining low-latency performance. This work is significant for deploying safer LLM systems in production environments.

**[PolicyShiftGuard: Benchmarking and Improving Policy-Adaptive Image Guardrails](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05910v1)** introduces a new paradigm for content moderation where safety policies can dynamically change during deployment. The work reveals that existing guardrails fail when policy boundaries shift and proposes PolicyShiftBench as a systematic evaluation framework. This addresses a critical gap in AI safety research by acknowledging that safety requirements evolve over time and vary across applications.

**[A Definition and Roadmap for World Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06401v1)** provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework for world models, establishing clear definitions and taxonomies for this rapidly growing field. The paper addresses the current fragmentation in world model research by proposing unified evaluation metrics and architectural principles. This foundational work is crucial for advancing the safety and reliability of AI systems that must understand and simulate complex environments.

**[TILDE: TILt-based Distributional Erasure for Concept Unlearning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06432v1)** tackles the critical challenge of removing unwanted concepts from trained diffusion models while preserving model quality on benign content. The method uses distributional erasure to maintain semantic coverage, addressing a key limitation of existing unlearning approaches that often degrade overall model performance. This work is essential for deploying generative models safely in production environments where certain concepts must be suppressed.

**[Pluralis v0.1: Towards a Multicultural, Multimodal, Multilingual Benchmark for AI Risk and Reliability](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06196v1)** exposes critical blind spots in AI safety evaluation by demonstrating that Western-centric benchmarks miss region-specific harms and cultural sensitivities. The benchmark spans 6,448 prompts across six Asia-Pacific countries, revealing significant performance gaps in vision-language models when evaluated on culturally-specific content. This work highlights the urgent need for globally inclusive safety evaluation frameworks.

**[X-FEMR: A Token-level Explainable Approach for Electronic Health Records Foundation Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06163v1)** addresses the black-box nature of foundation models in healthcare by introducing the first token-level explainability framework for electronic health records. The approach uses Transformer-based surrogate models to provide interpretable explanations for clinical predictions, which is crucial for building trust and enabling clinical adoption of AI systems in healthcare settings.

**[Controlling Tool Use with Heading-Specific Activation Steering](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05790v1)** demonstrates that tool-use decisions in LLMs have stable internal representations that can be extracted and manipulated, even though tools exist only in context at inference time. The work shows bidirectional control over tool invocation decisions, providing new insights into how LLMs represent and process contextual capabilities. This has important implications for controlling and debugging tool-augmented language models.

**[Beyond Refusal: A Same-Lineage Study of Aligned and Abliterated LLMs for Vulnerability Analysis](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05842v1)** conducts a controlled study comparing aligned and "abliterated" models from the same lineage to isolate safety behavior from other model differences. The research reveals that safety alignment can impede legitimate cybersecurity use cases, highlighting tensions between safety measures and beneficial applications. This work provides crucial empirical evidence for understanding the costs and benefits of different safety alignment approaches.
